Module 4
Blood Recycling — The Liver and Spleen Team
Questions 1 – 3
Question 1 of 10
How long does a red blood cell live before the spleen flags it for recycling?
Red blood cells live approximately 120 days. After that the spleen dismantles them and sends the heme fragment to the liver for conversion into bilirubin.
Question 2 of 10
When bilirubin cannot exit the body fast enough through bile, where does it go?
When bile flow is too slow or the liver is congested, bilirubin backs up into the bloodstream and begins depositing in tissues — first in the whites of the eyes, then on the skin. This is what jaundice looks like.
Question 3 of 10
⚡ Shocking truth question
Jaundice — yellowing of the skin and eyes — is best described as:
Jaundice is not a skin condition. It is the liver's backlog showing up on the body's surface. The source is in the blood recycling chain — bilirubin cannot exit fast enough through bile and deposits in the tissues instead. Common drivers: high protein load, pharmaceutical drugs, alcohol, and dehydration.
Module 5
Protein = Acid = Workload
Questions 4 – 6
Question 4 of 10
When unused amino acids are broken down (deaminated), what toxic substance is produced?
Deamination of unused amino acids produces ammonia — one of the most toxic substances the body makes. The liver then converts ammonia into urea, which the kidneys filter out through urine. Every protein source goes through this chain.
Question 5 of 10
⚡ Shocking truth question
Why does Dr. Morse call meat "second-hand protein"?
Dr. Morse calls meat second-hand protein because the body must demolish the entire protein structure — like tearing down a building to get to the bricks — before it can access simple amino acids. Plant proteins come pre-simplified and require far less processing. The extra work produces more ammonia, more acid, and more liver and kidney burden.
Question 6 of 10
What does Dr. Morse's "two-organ tax" refer to?
Every high-protein meal taxes two organs simultaneously: the liver converts ammonia into urea (processing burden), and the kidneys filter that urea out of the blood (filtration burden). Neither organ gets to rest when protein load is chronic and high.
Module 6
Why Kidneys Crash
Questions 7 – 10
Question 7 of 10
⚡ Shocking truth question
You do not feel nephron (kidney filtering unit) damage until you have lost how much kidney function?
The body compensates silently. You do not feel nephron damage until more than 60% of kidney function is compromised. By the time symptoms appear — fatigue, swelling, nausea, itching — the kidneys have been under strain for a long time. This is why terrain monitoring before labs change matters.
Question 8 of 10
Which of these is a symptom of uremia — nitrogenous waste building up in the bloodstream?
Uremia produces a cluster of symptoms: itchy skin (urea crystals trying to exit through skin), ammonia-smelling breath (urea crossing into lung air sacs), fatigue and brain fog (toxic blood affecting cellular function), and fluid retention and swelling (kidneys cannot excrete excess fluid). These symptoms are often treated separately — but they share one source.
Question 9 of 10
What does an elevated BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) number on a lab panel suggest?
Elevated BUN reflects high protein intake creating excess urea, or kidney compromise reducing how much urea is cleared, or both simultaneously. BUN is one of the clearest lab signals of protein overconsumption and kidney stress. The terrain approach asks what is producing the urea — and reduces the source.
Question 10 of 10
⚡ Shocking truth question
Creatinine is used as a kidney damage marker because:
Creatinine is produced from muscle activity and excreted entirely by the kidneys. Because it has no other exit route, when the nephrons are damaged and filtration slows — creatinine rises in the blood. Medicine waits until creatinine is significantly elevated before acting. The terrain model removes the burden before the labs catch up.
